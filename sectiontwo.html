Quantum Mechanics: Schrödinger's Cat, Superposition, and its Daily Presence

Enter Erwin Schrödinger who proposed a thought experiment which helps to further explain the copenhagen interpretation and helps to better understand the concept of superposition and the collapse of the wave function. In 1935, Schrödinger performed a thought experiment where he imagined a cat in a "quantum box". Inside this quantum box exists our special cat and a piece of radioactive material set to bust open and kill the cat or lie dormant and spare the cat's life. While the quantum box is unobserved the cat exists in both an alive state as well as a dead state at the same time. This simultaneous state is better known as superposition. When the quantum box is opened, the wave function collapses thus ceasing this superposition phenomena.

The idea of superposition ripples through many modern day electronic devices. The first modern devices which we will discuss that constantly employ quantum mechanics - specifically superposition, are semiconductors. Semiconductors are present in most every electronic device we interact with on a daily basis. A semiconductor's job is to either allow, or prevent the flow of electricity at any given point in time. Modern computers may have billions of these semiconductors working in unison to provide us with the fancy technology that makes our lives easier. One type of semiconductor is a transistor, and in its most basic sense it generates states of on or off. The electrons which are stored inside of a transistor are constantly shifting between certain energy states. This is superposition at work, and the outcome of this superposition determines the final on/off state of the transistor.

GPS technology relies on quantum mechanics as well! The way that our phones or other GPS devices accurately receive positions relies heavily on our good friend superposition. In this case Atomic clocks are also used to calculate positioning by taking the amount of time that a signal takes to travel between satellite and device, and when exactly this signal was sent from the satellite. Einstein's theory of general relativity helped to bring the Atomic Clock into existence, but what makes the Atomic Clock tick? The superposition of energy states between the different component atoms.

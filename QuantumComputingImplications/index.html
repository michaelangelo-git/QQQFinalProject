Quantum Mechanics: Quantum Computing, Cutting Edge, and Future Applications

The introduction of previous concepts has led us to a rather exciting point in modern technology. Now that we have a better understanding of quantum mechanics and its properties, we can delve into how it works, where this technology is at, and its potential in the near future. A quantum computer is based on quantum bits. A quantum bit or "Qubit" is no longer in a state of 0 or 1, but rather can be 0 or 1 as well as 1 and 0 at the same time. Once again this concept of superposition reigns supreme. The idea of a qubit has been brought to life by tech moguls such as IBM, and Alphabet Inc. (Google).

In 2017, IBM released IBM Q, which was the first of its kind. IBM Q held an astonishing 50 Qubits! Quantum computers require a near perfect environmental conditions to operate, one of which includes subzero temperatures. This cooling is necessary due to the thermal energy that is produced by the components of the computer. There also is a level of precision in design unmatched to classical computational devices. This is where the term quantum supremacy comes into play. A company is said to have quantum supremacy when their device outperforms other quantum computer in comparison to the performance of classical computers which are based on simply 0s and 1s.

In 2019 Alphabet Inc. announced quantum supremacy in a paper with its Sycamore Processor, which can out perform classical computers at astonishing rates. The Sycamore processor can achieve tasks in 200 seconds which would take a classical computer 10,000 years to achieve! However, as studies provide more insight we approach a new cutting edge of quantum computing advancements.

In 2020, quantum components have been created by a Sydney University which can operate at temperatures much hotter than IBM Q, and this allows for more money to be spent elsewhere in production. The current cutting edge of quantum computing remains in the hands of IBM Q System One, which was released in 2019 as the first commercial Quantum Computer. As Quantum computers become available for commercial use, a major point to consider is how exactly this technology can be applied in the future for both goverment use and private use.

The main application for future use of quantum computing by the government would be for cryptographic hasing functions. A cryptographic hash function is responsible for keeping all of our passcodes safe on the internet, as well as at many offices across the world! A quantum computer can also be publicly used as well in different areas. These areas include biology, especially when attempting to find certain patterns of genes in an organisms DNA make-up. What this can allow humanity to do is further explore the human genome and potentially solve questions that have been difficult to solve for decades, and construct new medicine. Another public application is also finding new information about physics. This can be done by taking adavntage of the quantum computer's ability to create quantum simulations. We are in a very interesting time, and this all wraps back to the inital Copenghagen interpretation and the steady improvement of our understanding of Quantum Mechanics.
